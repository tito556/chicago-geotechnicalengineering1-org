Electrical Resistivity Testing and VES Surveys in Chicago

Together, we solve the challenges of tomorrow.

SEE MORE →

The resistivity meter goes out before the drill rig when the question is what lies beneath 50 feet of glacial till without disturbing the gas lines and old freight tunnels that crisscross Chicago. We run a four-electrode array—Wenner or Schlumberger depending on the target depth—and inject current into the ground across the thin soil cover that overlies the Lake Michigan lakebed deposits. The voltage drop between the potential electrodes gives us apparent resistivity. We iterate the spacing outward. Each expansion pushes the current deeper, building a sounding curve that resolves the contact between the compressible Blodgett- Deerfield silty clay, the dense till, and the dolomitic bedrock. In the Loop, where surface access is tight, we often complement the survey with a MASW profile to cross-check the shear-wave velocity against the resistivity stratigraphy before anyone pours a mat foundation.

A sharp resistivity drop at 15 to 25 feet in Chicago almost always signals the lakebed clay contact—miss it, and your excavation support design starts from the wrong assumption.

Process overview

What we keep seeing across Cook County is a sharp resistivity drop around 15 to 25 feet depth—right where the natural moisture content jumps in the lakebed clays. In a recent survey near the old Chicago River meander, our VES curve showed a 12 ohm-m layer sitting directly above a 90 ohm-m unit. The borehole log later confirmed organic silt over sand. That kind of contrast makes resistivity a fast screening tool. The key is calibrating the raw apparent values against a test pit or SPT log, because the same 30 ohm-m reading can mean stiff clay on one block and loose saturated sand three blocks east. We run dipole-dipole arrays for lateral profiling when the client needs to map a buried channel or an old demolition trench. The data processing flow includes smoothness-constrained inversion, and we deliver a 2D resistivity section with RMS error below 5%. Every sounding gets a written interpretation tied to USCS soil types.

Local context

Chicago’s building code (Title 14B) references IBC Chapter 18, which requires classification of the site soil profile down to bedrock or 100 ft. Guessing the depth to the dolomite across the Chicago Lake Plain based on adjacent lots is a gamble—glacial erosion carved the bedrock surface into ridges and valleys that can drop 30 ft within a single city block. If the till cover is thin over a bedrock high, a driven pile designed for a 70 ft socket might refuse at 40 ft, throwing the load test off schedule. The other risk we flag is undetected saturated sand lenses inside the till; their low resistivity signature appears before the CPT cone hits them, and they are the layers that trigger base instability during deep excavation. We map those lenses with the resistivity line so the dewatering plan accounts for them.

Additional services

01

Vertical Electrical Sounding (VES)

A single-point sounding with expanding electrode spacing, ideal for determining depth to bedrock, clay thickness, and the base of the compressible lakebed deposits. We use the Schlumberger array for better vertical resolution and deliver a layered resistivity model calibrated to nearby boring logs.

02

2D Electrical Resistivity Tomography (ERT)

Multi-electrode profile along a line, typically 200 to 400 ft, processed into a true resistivity cross-section. Applied for mapping abandoned sewer trenches, buried foundations, groundwater plumes, and lateral changes in the till blanket that affect earth retention design.

Top questions

How deep can an electrical resistivity survey see in Chicago’s glacial soils?

With a standard 200 ft cable spread and the Schlumberger array, a VES sounding reliably images down to 80-100 ft below grade in the Chicago Lake Plain. The practical depth limit is controlled by the maximum current we can inject and the background electrical noise from the L trains and underground utilities. In the Loop, we often switch to nighttime surveys to reduce cultural noise and reach the dolomite bedrock contact, which sits anywhere from 40 to 100 ft deep.

Can electrical resistivity detect underground utilities before drilling?

Yes, partially. A metallic pipe or a concrete-encased duct bank shows as a very low-resistivity anomaly on a 2D ERT line. We run a dipole-dipole profile along the proposed boring alignment before mobilizing the drill rig, especially in downtown Chicago where old freight tunnels and abandoned gas lines are common. The method does not replace a full utility locate, but it catches buried structures that miss the One Call ticket because they are privately owned or predate modern records.

What does a VES survey cost in the Chicago area?

A single VES sounding with data processing and interpretation typically runs between US$590 and US$940, depending on the target depth and site accessibility. A 2D ERT line along 400 ft with 5 ft electrode spacing falls in the same range per line. The final number depends on how many soundings or line-feet are needed, whether we must work at night due to traffic or electrical noise, and whether the client requires a rush report.

How do you convert resistivity values to soil types?

We do not convert resistivity directly into a USCS classification—that would be misleading. Resistivity responds to clay content, porosity, and pore-water conductivity, not grain size alone. A stiff Chicago blue clay and a loose silty sand can both read around 30 ohm-m under different moisture conditions. Our approach is to calibrate the resistivity section against at least one nearby boring or test pit log, then use the resistivity contrasts to map the boundaries between units. The final deliverable gives you layer thicknesses and lateral continuity, with soil descriptions tied to the physical log, not the resistivity value.
